Uniform Inference on Relative Treatment Effects

Author

David Malison

Last modified

July 14, 2026

Average treatment effects (ATEs) are often reported relative to the baseline mean. This note studies how to conduct robust statistical inference on this ratio. We first show why scaling the ATE confidence interval or applying the delta method yields confidence intervals with unreliable coverage. We then construct a uniformly valid confidence set by combining Fieller’s method with a test of the baseline mean’s sign. We also show how to use a similar construction when effects are reported relative to a full-population baseline. 1

Relative treatment effect

We continue working with the fixed potential outcomes model introduced in the treatment group means note. We assume throughout this note that \(\overline{y}_{0,n} \neq 0\) for all \(n\). Let \[ \psi_n^{\mathrm{ATE}} = \overline{y}_{1,n} - \overline{y}_{0,n} \] denote the average treatment effect (ATE). It is common to report the ATE relative to the control mean \(\overline{y}_{0,n}\). Define the relative treatment effect (or treatment effect lift) as \[ \rho_n = \frac{\psi_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}{|\overline{y}_{0,n}|} = \frac{\overline{y}_{1,n} - \overline{y}_{0,n}}{|\overline{y}_{0,n}|}. \tag{1}\]

The absolute value in the denominator ensures \(\rho_n\) and \(\psi_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}\) share the same sign. Our goal in this note is to construct an asymptotically valid confidence set for \(\rho_n\).

Scaled ATE confidence interval

A simple but naive confidence interval for \(\rho_n\) can be obtained by scaling the ATE confidence interval by \(1/|\widehat{y}_{0,n}|\). This approach yields the interval

\[ C_{1-\alpha,n}^{\mathrm{scaled}} = \left[ \widehat{\rho}_n - z_{1-\alpha/2}\widehat{\mathrm{se}}^{\,\mathrm{scaled}}_n, \widehat{\rho}_n + z_{1-\alpha/2}\widehat{\mathrm{se}}^{\,\mathrm{scaled}}_n \right] \tag{2}\] where

\[ \begin{gathered} \widehat{\rho}_n = \frac{\widehat{y}_{1,n} - \widehat{y}_{0,n}}{|\widehat{y}_{0,n}|}, \qquad \widehat{\mathrm{se}}^{\,\mathrm{scaled}}_n = \frac{\widehat{\sigma}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}{\sqrt{n}\,|\widehat{y}_{0,n}|},\\[1ex] \widehat{\sigma}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}} = \widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}+\widehat{\sigma}_{1,n}, \qquad \widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}^2 = \frac{\overline{D}_n\,\widehat{S}_{0,n}^2}{1-\overline{D}_n}, \qquad \widehat{\sigma}_{1,n}^2 = \frac{(1-\overline{D}_n)\,\widehat{S}_{1,n}^2}{\overline{D}_n}. \end{gathered} \]

The problem with this interval is that it effectively ignores sampling variability in the denominator. As a consequence, its asymptotic coverage does not equal its nominal level in general.

Figure 1 illustrates this behavior. We simulated \(10{,}000\) draws from a \(N(0,1)\) distribution and normalized them to have exactly zero mean and unit variance. We then set the potential outcomes for each unit according to \[ \begin{aligned} y_{i,0} &= 1 + \epsilon_i, \\[1ex] y_{i,1} &= 1 + \rho_n + \epsilon_i, \end{aligned} \tag{3}\] where \(\epsilon_i\) is the normalized draw for unit \(i\). The top two panels plot the sampling distribution of \[ \frac{\widehat{\rho}_n - \rho_n} {\widehat{\mathrm{se}}^{\,\mathrm{scaled}}_n} \] for \(\rho_n=1\%\) and \(\rho_n=100\%\). The bottom panel plots the empirical non-coverage as a function of \(\rho_n\), with the two lift values from the top panels marked in their corresponding histogram colors.

At \(\rho_n=1\%\), the studentized statistic is close to \(N(0,1)\); at \(\rho_n=100\%\), however, the statistic is visibly over-dispersed. The bottom panel shows that non-coverage matches the nominal level only when \(\rho_n=0\). For positive lifts, the interval is too narrow; for negative lifts, the interval is too wide.

Delta method

To address the coverage problem illustrated in Figure 1, we need to incorporate denominator uncertainty into inference. One approach is to apply the delta method to

\[ \sqrt{n}\left(\widehat{\rho}_n - \rho_n\right). \tag{4}\]

Taking a first-order Taylor expansion of (1) around \((\overline{y}_{0,n}, \overline{y}_{1,n})\) and evaluating it at \((\widehat{y}_{0,n}, \widehat{y}_{1,n})\) gives

\[ \sqrt{n}\left(\widehat{\rho}_n - \rho_n\right) = \frac{1}{|\overline{y}_{0,n}|} \sqrt n\left[ \left(\widehat{y}_{1,n}-\overline{y}_{1,n}\right) - \left(1+\operatorname{sgn}(\overline{y}_{0,n})\rho_n\right) \left(\widehat{y}_{0,n}-\overline{y}_{0,n}\right) \right] + R_n \tag{5}\] where \(R_n\) is the Taylor remainder. This expansion yields the delta method confidence interval \[ C_{1-\alpha,n}^{\mathrm{DM}} = \left[ \widehat{\rho}_n - z_{1-\alpha/2}\widehat{\mathrm{se}}^{\,\mathrm{DM}}_n, \widehat{\rho}_n + z_{1-\alpha/2}\widehat{\mathrm{se}}^{\,\mathrm{DM}}_n \right] \tag{6}\] where \[ \widehat{\mathrm{se}}^{\,\mathrm{DM}}_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt n\,|\widehat{y}_{0,n}|} \left( \left|1+\operatorname{sgn}(\widehat{y}_{0,n})\widehat{\rho}_n\right|\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n} + \widehat{\sigma}_{1,n} \right). \]

If we compare (2) with (6), we see that the delta method standard error replaces the control-mean contribution \(\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}\) with \(\left|1+\operatorname{sgn}(\widehat{y}_{0,n})\widehat{\rho}_n\right|\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}\). This difference explains the pattern in Figure 1: the two intervals are nearly the same when \(\rho_n\) is close to zero, while the scaled ATE confidence interval understates uncertainty for positive lifts and overstates it for negative lifts because the baseline mean is positive.

The problem with the delta method is that we cannot uniformly control the remainder \(R_n\) in (5) without further restrictions. As a consequence, the delta method interval is not uniformly valid over the parameter space. Along sequences where the denominator approaches zero, coverage can be poor.

Figure 2 illustrates this behavior. To generate the data for this figure, we set the potential outcomes for each unit according to \[ \begin{aligned} y_{i, 0} &= \overline{y}_{0,n} + \epsilon_i, \\[1ex] y_{i, 1} &= 1.1 \overline{y}_{0,n} + \epsilon_i \end{aligned} \tag{7}\] The top two panels in the figure again plot the sampling distribution of the standardized statistic. The left figure displays the distribution for \(\overline{y}_{0,n} = 0.01\), while the right figure displays the distribution for \(\overline{y}_{0,n} = 0.1\). The bottom panel plots the empirical non-coverage of the delta-method confidence interval against the number of standard errors \(\sqrt{n}\,\overline{y}_{0,n}/\sigma_{0,n}\) separating the baseline from zero.

At \(\overline{y}_{0,n}=0.01\) the delta-method non-coverage is well above the nominal level, while at \(\overline{y}_{0,n}=0.1\) the standardized statistic is close to \(N(0,1)\) and non-coverage is near nominal. The bottom panel shows that the delta method only achieves nominal coverage when the baseline mean is more than 10 standard errors from zero.

Fieller’s method

To perform robust inference, we need a coverage guarantee that holds uniformly over the parameter space. In the treatment group means note, we showed how to construct such confidence sets for a linear combination of means. In this section, we leverage this result to construct a robust confidence set for \(\rho_n\) without introducing further restrictions on the parameter space.

Splitting by the control mean sign

We first eliminate the absolute value in the definition of \(\rho_n\) by defining \[ \rho_n^+ = \frac{\psi_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}{\overline{y}_{0,n}},\qquad \rho_n^- = -\frac{\psi_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}{\overline{y}_{0,n}} \] We construct confidence sets for \(\rho_n^+\) and \(\rho_n^-\) using Fieller’s method. Define \(\psi_n^+\) and \(\psi_n^-\) by \[ \psi_n^+ = (1+\rho_n^+)\,\overline{y}_{0,n} - \overline{y}_{1,n}, \qquad \psi_n^- = \overline{y}_{1,n} - (1-\rho_n^-)\,\overline{y}_{0,n}, \] with sample analogues \(\widehat{\psi}_n^+\) and \(\widehat{\psi}_n^-\). For any nominal level \(1-\alpha\), let \(C^+_{1-\alpha,n}\) and \(C^-_{1-\alpha,n}\) denote the confidence sets for \(\psi_n^+\) and \(\psi_n^-\) defined in the treatment group means note. For \(s \in \{+,-\}\), these confidence sets satisfy \[ \liminf_{n\to \infty}\inf_{\theta \in \Theta}\mathbb{P}_\theta(\psi_n^s \in C_{1-\alpha,n}^s) \geq 1-\alpha \]

By construction, \[ \left\{\psi_n^s \in C_{1-\alpha,n}^s\right\} \iff \left\{\left|\frac{\widehat{\psi}_n^s - \psi_n^s}{\sqrt{(\widehat{\sigma}_n^{s}(\theta))^2/n}}\right| \leq z_{1-\alpha/2}\right\} \] where \[ \begin{aligned} (\widehat{\sigma}_n^{+}(\theta))^2 &= \left(\left|1+\rho_n^+\right|\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n} + \widehat{\sigma}_{1,n}\right)^2, \\[1ex] (\widehat{\sigma}_n^{-}(\theta))^2 &= \left(\left|1-\rho_n^-\right|\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n} + \widehat{\sigma}_{1,n}\right)^2 \end{aligned} \]

For any candidate value \(r\), the sample analogues simplify to \[ \widehat{\psi}_n^+(r) = \widehat{y}_{0,n}\,r - \widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}, \qquad \widehat{\psi}_n^-(r) = \widehat{y}_{0,n}\,r + \widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}. \] At the true values of \(\rho_n^+\) and \(\rho_n^-\), we also have \[\psi_n^+ = \psi_n^- = 0.\] We can therefore write \[ \begin{aligned} &\left\{\left| \frac{\widehat{\psi}_n^+ - \psi_n^+} {\sqrt{(\widehat{\sigma}_n^{+}(\theta))^2/n}} \right| \leq z_{1-\alpha/2}\right\} \iff \left\{\left| \frac{\widehat{y}_{0,n}\,\rho_n^+ - \widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}} {\left(\left|1+\rho_n^+\right|\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n} + \widehat{\sigma}_{1,n}\right)/\sqrt n} \right| \leq z_{1-\alpha/2}\right\}, \\[1ex] &\left\{\left| \frac{\widehat{\psi}_n^- - \psi_n^-} {\sqrt{(\widehat{\sigma}_n^{-}(\theta))^2/n}} \right| \leq z_{1-\alpha/2}\right\} \iff \left\{\left| \frac{\widehat{y}_{0,n}\,\rho_n^- + \widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}} {\left(\left|1-\rho_n^-\right|\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n} + \widehat{\sigma}_{1,n}\right)/\sqrt n} \right| \leq z_{1-\alpha/2}\right\}. \end{aligned} \tag{8}\]

Inverting the right-hand side expressions, we obtain the confidence sets \[ \begin{aligned} C_{1-\alpha,n}^{+} &= \Bigg\{r :{} \left(\widehat{y}_{0,n}^2 - \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}^2\right)r^2 \\ &\qquad - 2\left(\widehat{y}_{0,n}\,\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}} + \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}^2\right)r - \frac{2z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{1,n}\left(\left|1+r\right| - 1\right) \\ &\qquad + (\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}})^2 - \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,(\widehat{\sigma}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}})^2 \leq 0\Bigg\}, \\[2ex] C_{1-\alpha,n}^{-} &= \Bigg\{r :{} \left(\widehat{y}_{0,n}^2 - \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}^2\right)r^2 \\ &\qquad + 2\left(\widehat{y}_{0,n}\,\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}} + \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}^2\right)r - \frac{2z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{1,n}\left(\left|1-r\right| - 1\right) \\ &\qquad + (\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}})^2 - \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,(\widehat{\sigma}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}})^2 \leq 0\Bigg\}. \end{aligned} \tag{9}\]

The expressions on the left-hand side of the inequalities in (9) are piecewise quadratic. Each piece has leading coefficient

\[ \widehat{y}_{0,n}^2 - \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}^2. \]

If this coefficient is positive, the sign-specific confidence set is a bounded interval; otherwise, it contains values that are arbitrarily large in magnitude. Equivalently, the confidence sets are bounded intervals if and only if \[ \frac{|\widehat{y}_{0,n}|}{\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n} / \sqrt{n}} > z_{1-\alpha/2}. \tag{10}\] The expression in (10) is exactly the condition for rejecting the null hypothesis that the control mean is zero at level \(\alpha\). Thus, if we cannot bound the control mean away from zero at level \(\alpha\), we also cannot obtain a bounded confidence interval for \(\rho_n^s\) at this confidence level.

Evaluating the inequalities in (9) at \(r=0\) shows that \(0 \in C_{1-\alpha,n}^{s}\) if and only if \[ (\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}})^2 - \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,(\widehat{\sigma}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}})^2 \leq 0, \] or equivalently, \[ |\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}| \leq z_{1-\alpha/2}\,\frac{\widehat{\sigma}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}{\sqrt n}. \] Thus zero belongs to the sign-specific Fieller set exactly when the usual two-sided ATE test fails to reject at level \(\alpha\).

Lastly, note that the numerators on the right-hand side of (8) equal zero at \(\rho_n^s = \widehat{\rho}_n^s\), where \[ \widehat{\rho}_n^+ = \frac{\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}{\widehat{y}_{0,n}}, \qquad \widehat{\rho}_n^- = -\frac{\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}{\widehat{y}_{0,n}}. \] As a consequence, these sign-specific point estimates always lie in their respective confidence sets. However, they need not correspond to the confidence set midpoints.

Figure 3 and Figure 4 illustrate the confidence sets in (9). In each panel, the shaded region marks the values of \(\rho_n^s\) for which the corresponding Fieller inequality value in (9) is below zero.

Figure 3 displays the bounded case. The Fieller inequality value is positive in both tails, so each sign-specific confidence set is an interval around the corresponding point estimate. Note that the point estimate is not the midpoint of the interval.

Figure 4 displays the unbounded case. The Fieller inequality value is negative in the tails, so the confidence set contains arbitrarily large positive or negative lifts.

It is useful to compare these confidence sets with the delta-method confidence interval in (6). For the positive-denominator region, (9) can be written as

\[ \left| \rho_n^+ - \frac{\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}{\widehat{y}_{0,n}} \right| \leq \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}}{\sqrt n\,|\widehat{y}_{0,n}|} \left( \left|1+\rho_n^+\right|\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n} + \widehat{\sigma}_{1,n} \right). \tag{11}\]

When \(\widehat{y}_{0,n} > 0\), the only difference from the interval in (6) is that the right-hand side of (11) substitutes \(\rho_n^+\) for \(\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}/\widehat{y}_{0,n}\). A similar expression holds for the negative-denominator region. In the Appendix, we show that substitution has a negligible impact on the confidence set when the baseline mean is well-separated from zero.

Confidence interval for \(\rho_n\)

Known baseline sign

We now combine the sign-specific sets into a confidence interval for \(\rho_n\). First suppose the sign of \(\overline{y}_{0,n}\) is known to be positive. In this case, \(\rho_n=\rho_n^+\), so we could directly use the positive-denominator Fieller set. As illustrated in Figure 4, however, the sign-specific confidence set may not be an interval when the denominator is not statistically distinguishable from zero. To obtain a connected set, choose \(\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}>0\) with \(\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}+\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}=\alpha\) and \(\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}\leq\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}\). Let \(C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{+}\) denote the positive-denominator Fieller set, and define the denominator and ATE sign statistics

\[ T_{0,n} = \frac{\sqrt n\,\widehat{y}_{0,n}}{\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}}, \qquad T_{\Delta,n} = \frac{\sqrt n\,\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}{\widehat{\sigma}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}. \tag{12}\]

Consider the sign-specific interval

\[ I_{1-\alpha,n}^{+} = \begin{cases} C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{+}, & |T_{0,n}|>z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}/2}, \\[3ex] C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{+}\cap[0,\infty), & |T_{0,n}|\leq z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}/2}, \quad T_{\Delta,n}>z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2}, \\[3ex] C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{+}\cap(-\infty,0], & |T_{0,n}|\leq z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}/2}, \quad T_{\Delta,n}<-z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2}, \\[3ex] \mathbb{R}, & |T_{0,n}|\leq z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}/2}, \quad |T_{\Delta,n}|\leq z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2}. \end{cases} \tag{13}\]

When the denominator is statistically distinguishable from zero, the Fieller set is bounded and we use it directly. When the denominator is not statistically distinguishable from zero, we use an ATE sign test. If the ATE is significantly positive, we truncate the Fieller set to nonnegative values; if it is significantly negative, we truncate to nonpositive values. If the ATE sign test is inconclusive, we return the real line. The condition \(\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}\leq\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}\) ensures that, when truncation occurs, the resulting set is a ray rather than two disjoint pieces.

To see that \(I_{1-\alpha,n}^{+}\) remains uniformly valid, let \[ E_{\mathrm{Fieller}} = \left\{ \rho_n^+\in C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{+} \right\} \] and let \(E_{\mathrm{sign}}\) denote the event that the ATE sign test is either inconclusive or selects the correct sign: \[ E_{\mathrm{sign}} = \left\{|T_{\Delta,n}|\leq z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2}\right\} \cup \left\{T_{\Delta,n}>z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2},\;\psi_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}>0\right\} \cup \left\{T_{\Delta,n}<-z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2},\;\psi_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}<0\right\}. \] On the event \(E_{\mathrm{Fieller}}\cap E_{\mathrm{sign}}\), the Fieller set contains \(\rho_n\) and truncation based on the sign test does not remove it. Therefore \[ \mathbb{P}_\theta\{\rho_n \in I_{1-\alpha,n}^{+}\} \geq \mathbb{P}_\theta(E_{\mathrm{Fieller}}\cap E_{\mathrm{sign}}) \geq 1 - \mathbb{P}_\theta\{E_{\mathrm{Fieller}}^c\} - \mathbb{P}_\theta\{E_{\mathrm{sign}}^c\} \] which implies \[ \begin{aligned} \liminf_{n\to\infty}\inf_{\theta \in \Theta}\mathbb{P}_\theta\{\rho_n \in I_{1-\alpha,n}^{+}\} &\geq 1 - \limsup_{n\to\infty}\sup_{\theta \in \Theta}\mathbb{P}_\theta\{E_{\mathrm{Fieller}}^c\} - \limsup_{n\to\infty}\sup_{\theta \in \Theta}\mathbb{P}_\theta\{E_{\mathrm{sign}}^c\} \\[1ex] &\geq 1 - \alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}} - \alpha^{\mathrm{sign}} \\[1ex] &= 1 - \alpha. \end{aligned} \]

If \(\overline{y}_{0,n}<0\) is known instead, define \(I_{1-\alpha,n}^{-}\) analogously by replacing \(C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{+}\) with \(C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{-}\) in (13). The same argument gives uniform validity.

Unknown baseline sign

In the case when the sign of \(\overline{y}_{0,n}\) is unknown, we can use the denominator statistic \(T_{0,n}\) in (12) to determine which sign-specific set to use. Choose \(\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}>0\) with \(\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}+\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}=\alpha\) and \(\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}\leq\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}\). Define the confidence set for \(\rho_n\) as

\[ C_{1-\alpha,n} = \begin{cases} C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{+}, & T_{0,n}>z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2}, \\[1ex] C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{-}, & T_{0,n}<-z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2}, \\[1ex] \mathbb{R}, & |T_{0,n}|\leq z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2}. \end{cases} \tag{14}\]

If \(T_{0,n}\) is significantly positive at level \(\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}} / 2\), \(C_{1-\alpha,n}\) uses the positive-denominator set; if it is significantly negative, \(C_{1-\alpha,n}\) uses the negative-denominator set. If the sign test is inconclusive, \(C_{1-\alpha,n}\) reverts to the real line. Because \(\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}\leq\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}\), rejection of the denominator sign test implies the selected sign-specific Fieller set is a bounded interval.

To prove \(C_{1-\alpha,n}\) is uniformly asymptotically valid, partition the sample space by the three possible outcomes of the sign test:

\[ E_{0,+} = \left\{ T_{0,n} > z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2} \right\}, \qquad E_{0,-} = \left\{ T_{0,n} < -z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2} \right\}, \qquad E_{0} = \left\{ \left|T_{0,n}\right|\leq z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2} \right\}. \]

Let

\[ E_+ = \left\{ \rho_n^+\in C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{+} \right\}, \qquad E_- = \left\{ \rho_n^-\in C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{-} \right\}. \] denote the coverage events for the sign-specific confidence sets.

First suppose \(\overline{y}_{0,n}>0\). If \(E_{0,+}\cap E_+\) occurs, then \(C_{1-\alpha,n}=C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{+}\) and \(\rho_n=\rho_n^+\), so \(\rho_n\in C_{1-\alpha,n}\). If \(E_{0}\) occurs, then \(C_{1-\alpha,n}=\mathbb{R}\), so \(\rho_n\in C_{1-\alpha,n}\) automatically. Therefore

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{P}_\theta\left(\rho_n\in C_{1-\alpha,n}\right) &\geq \mathbb{P}_\theta\left((E_{0,+}\cap E_+)\cup E_{0}\right) \\[1ex] &= \mathbb{P}_\theta\left(E_{0,-}^c \cap (E_{0}\cup E_+)\right) \\[1ex] &= 1-\mathbb{P}_\theta\left(E_{0,-}\cup (E_{0}^c\cap E_+^c)\right) \\[1ex] &\geq 1-\mathbb{P}_\theta(E_{0,-})-\mathbb{P}_\theta(E_+^c). \end{aligned} \tag{15}\]

Since \(\overline{y}_{0,n}>0\),

\[ \begin{aligned} E_{0,-} &= \left\{ T_{0,n}<-z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2} \right\} \\ &\subseteq \left\{ \sqrt n \frac{\widehat{y}_{0,n}-\overline{y}_{0,n}} {\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}} <-z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2} \right\} \\ &\subseteq \left\{ \left| \sqrt n \frac{\widehat{y}_{0,n}-\overline{y}_{0,n}} {\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}} \right| > z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2} \right\}, \end{aligned} \tag{16}\]

Combining the inclusion in (16) with the bound in (15) gives

\[ \mathbb{P}_\theta\left( \rho_n\notin C_{1-\alpha,n} \right) \leq \mathbb{P}_\theta\left( \left| \sqrt n \frac{\widehat{y}_{0,n}-\overline{y}_{0,n}} {\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}} \right| > z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2} \right) + \mathbb{P}_\theta(E_+^c). \]

The same argument with signs reversed shows that, if \(\overline{y}_{0,n}<0\),

\[ \mathbb{P}_\theta\left( \rho_n\notin C_{1-\alpha,n} \right) \leq \mathbb{P}_\theta\left( \left| \sqrt n \frac{\widehat{y}_{0,n}-\overline{y}_{0,n}} {\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}} \right| > z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2} \right) + \mathbb{P}_\theta(E_-^c). \]

Therefore, for every \(n\) and \(\theta\in\Theta\),

\[ \begin{aligned} &\mathbb{P}_\theta\left( \rho_n\notin C_{1-\alpha,n} \right) \\ &\qquad\leq \mathbb{P}_\theta\left( \left| \sqrt n \frac{\widehat{y}_{0,n}-\overline{y}_{0,n}} {\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}} \right| > z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2} \right) + \max\left\{ \mathbb{P}_\theta(E_+^c), \mathbb{P}_\theta(E_-^c) \right\}. \end{aligned} \]

Taking the supremum over \(\theta\in\Theta\) gives

\[ \begin{aligned} &\sup_{\theta\in\Theta} \mathbb{P}_\theta\left( \rho_n\notin C_{1-\alpha,n} \right) \\ &\qquad\leq \sup_{\theta\in\Theta} \mathbb{P}_\theta\left( \left| \sqrt n \frac{\widehat{y}_{0,n}-\overline{y}_{0,n}} {\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}} \right| > z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2} \right) + \max\left\{ \sup_{\theta\in\Theta}\mathbb{P}_\theta(E_+^c), \sup_{\theta\in\Theta}\mathbb{P}_\theta(E_-^c) \right\}. \end{aligned} \]

The first term is controlled by the asymptotic validity of the control group mean confidence set. The maximum term is controlled by the asymptotic validity of the sign-specific confidence sets. Thus

\[ \limsup_{n\to\infty} \sup_{\theta\in\Theta:\,\overline{y}_{0,n}\neq0} \mathbb{P}_\theta\left( \rho_n\notin C_{1-\alpha,n} \right) \leq \alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}+\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}} = \alpha. \]

Equivalently,

\[ \liminf_{n\to\infty} \inf_{\theta\in\Theta:\,\overline{y}_{0,n}\neq0} \mathbb{P}_\theta\left( \rho_n\in C_{1-\alpha,n} \right) \geq 1-\alpha. \]

Therefore \(\{C_{1-\alpha,n}\}_{n \geq 1}\) is asymptotically valid for \(\{\rho_n\}_{n \geq 1}\) over \(\Theta\).

Unlike the scaled-ATE confidence set, the uniform set maintains nominal coverage across the range of lifts. The bottom panel varies the baseline at a fixed lift \(\rho_n=10\%\). Full-population relative effects

The units in an experiment often only represent a subset of a larger population of interest. For example, the experimental units may correspond to a set of users who qualified for a promotion, while the population of interest is all product users. In such settings, cross-experiment comparisons are easier if treatment effects are reported relative to a full-population baseline rather than to a particular experiment’s control group.

Let \(N_n^{\mathrm{untreated}}\) denote the number of units outside the experiment, and let \(\overline{y}_n^{\mathrm{untreated}}\) denote their mean outcome. We assume outcomes for untreated units are unaffected by the experiment’s randomization and are therefore held fixed. For \(d\in\{0,1\}\), the full-population mean under treatment state \(d\) is given by

\[ \overline{y}^\mathrm{full}_{d,n} = \frac{n}{N_n^{\mathrm{untreated}} + n}\overline{y}_{d, n} + \frac{N_n^{\mathrm{untreated}}}{N_n^{\mathrm{untreated}} + n} \overline{y}^{\mathrm{untreated}}_n \]

The full-population average treatment effect is defined as

\[ \psi_n^{\mathrm{full}} = \overline{y}^\mathrm{full}_{1,n} - \overline{y}^\mathrm{full}_{0,n} = \frac{n}{N_n^{\mathrm{untreated}} + n} \psi_n^{\mathrm{ATE}} \]

and the full-population relative effect is given by

\[ \rho_n^{\mathrm{full}} = \frac{\psi_n^{\mathrm{full}}}{|\overline{y}_{0,n}^{\mathrm{full}}|} \tag{17}\]

To construct a confidence set for \(\rho_n^{\mathrm{full}}\), define

\[ q_n = \frac{N_n^{\mathrm{untreated}}}{n}, \qquad B_n = \overline{y}_{0,n}+q_n\overline{y}_n^{\mathrm{untreated}}, \qquad \widehat{B}_n = \widehat{y}_{0,n}+q_n\overline{y}_n^{\mathrm{untreated}}. \]

Dividing the numerator and denominator in (17) by \(n/(N_n^{\mathrm{untreated}}+n)\) gives \[ \rho_n^{\mathrm{full}}=\frac{\psi_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}{|B_n|} \tag{18}\] Since

\[ \sqrt n(\widehat{B}_n-B_n)=\sqrt n(\widehat{y}_{0,n}-\overline{y}_{0,n}), \]

the standard error term for the denominator in (18) equals \(\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}/\sqrt n\). Now define the sign-specific targets

\[ \rho_n^{\mathrm{full},+}=\frac{\psi_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}{B_n}, \qquad \rho_n^{\mathrm{full},-}=-\frac{\psi_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}{B_n}. \]

and the corresponding linear combinations \(\psi_n^{\mathrm{full},+}\) and \(\psi_n^{\mathrm{full},-}\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \psi_n^{\mathrm{full},+} &= \rho_n^{\mathrm{full},+}B_n-\psi_n^{\mathrm{ATE}} \\[1ex] &= \left(1+\rho_n^{\mathrm{full},+}\right)\overline{y}_{0,n} -\overline{y}_{1,n} +\rho_n^{\mathrm{full},+}q_n\overline{y}_n^{\mathrm{untreated}}, \\[1ex] \psi_n^{\mathrm{full},-} &= \rho_n^{\mathrm{full},-}B_n+\psi_n^{\mathrm{ATE}} \\[1ex] &= \overline{y}_{1,n} -\left(1-\rho_n^{\mathrm{full},-}\right)\overline{y}_{0,n} +\rho_n^{\mathrm{full},-}q_n\overline{y}_n^{\mathrm{untreated}}. \end{aligned} \]

At the true values of \(\rho_n^{\mathrm{full},+}\) and \(\rho_n^{\mathrm{full},-}\), we again have \(\psi_n^{\mathrm{full},+}=\psi_n^{\mathrm{full},-}=0\). For any candidate value \(r\), the sample analogues simplify to \(\widehat{B}_n r-\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}\) and \(\widehat{B}_n r+\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}\). Define the sign-specific confidence sets as

\[ \begin{aligned} C_{1-\alpha,n}^{\mathrm{full},+} &= \left\{ r: \left|\widehat{B}_n r-\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}\right| \leq \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}}{\sqrt n} \left(\left|1+r\right|\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}+\widehat{\sigma}_{1,n}\right) \right\}, \\[1ex] C_{1-\alpha,n}^{\mathrm{full},-} &= \left\{ r: \left|\widehat{B}_n r+\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}\right| \leq \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}}{\sqrt n} \left(\left|1-r\right|\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}+\widehat{\sigma}_{1,n}\right) \right\}. \end{aligned} \tag{19}\]

Inverting these sets, we obtain

\[ \begin{aligned} C_{1-\alpha,n}^{\mathrm{full},+} &= \Bigg\{r :{} \left(\widehat{B}_n^2 - \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}^2\right)r^2 \\ &\qquad - 2\left(\widehat{B}_n\,\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}} + \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}^2\right)r - \frac{2z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{1,n}\left(\left|1+r\right| - 1\right) \\ &\qquad + (\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}})^2 - \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,(\widehat{\sigma}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}})^2 \leq 0\Bigg\}, \\[2ex] C_{1-\alpha,n}^{\mathrm{full},-} &= \Bigg\{r :{} \left(\widehat{B}_n^2 - \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}^2\right)r^2 \\ &\qquad + 2\left(\widehat{B}_n\,\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}} + \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}^2\right)r - \frac{2z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}\,\widehat{\sigma}_{1,n}\left(\left|1-r\right| - 1\right) \\ &\qquad + (\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}})^2 - \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}^2}{n}\,(\widehat{\sigma}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}})^2 \leq 0\Bigg\}. \end{aligned} \tag{20}\]

These confidence sets have the same form as the confidence sets in (9), with \(\widehat{B}_n\) replacing \(\widehat{y}_{0,n}\).

As in the control-baseline case, the sign-specific Fieller sets may fail to be intervals when the denominator is not statistically distinguishable from zero. Define the full-population denominator statistic \[ T_{0,n}^{\mathrm{full}} = \frac{\sqrt n\,\widehat{B}_n}{\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}}. \] Define the known-positive-baseline interval

\[ I_{1-\alpha,n}^{\mathrm{full},+} = \begin{cases} C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{\mathrm{full},+}, & |T_{0,n}^{\mathrm{full}}|>z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}/2}, \\[3ex] C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{\mathrm{full},+}\cap[0,\infty), & |T_{0,n}^{\mathrm{full}}|\leq z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}/2}, \quad T_{\Delta,n}>z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2}, \\[3ex] C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{\mathrm{full},+}\cap(-\infty,0], & |T_{0,n}^{\mathrm{full}}|\leq z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}/2}, \quad T_{\Delta,n}<-z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2}, \\[3ex] \mathbb{R}, & |T_{0,n}^{\mathrm{full}}|\leq z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}}/2}, \quad |T_{\Delta,n}|\leq z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2}. \end{cases} \tag{21}\]

If \(B_n>0\) is known, use \(I_{1-\alpha,n}^{\mathrm{full},+}\) as the confidence interval for \(\rho_n^{\mathrm{full}}\). If \(B_n<0\) is known, define \(I_{1-\alpha,n}^{\mathrm{full},-}\) analogously by replacing \(C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{\mathrm{full},+}\) with \(C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{\mathrm{full},-}\) in (21), and use \(I_{1-\alpha,n}^{\mathrm{full},-}\).

In the case when the sign of \(B_n\) is unknown, use the statistic \(T_{0,n}^{\mathrm{full}}\) to determine which sign-specific set to use:

\[ C_{1-\alpha,n}^{\mathrm{full}} = \begin{cases} C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{\mathrm{full},+}, & T_{0,n}^{\mathrm{full}}>z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2}, \\[1ex] C_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{Fiellers}},n}^{\mathrm{full},-}, & T_{0,n}^{\mathrm{full}}<-z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2}, \\[1ex] \mathbb{R}, & |T_{0,n}^{\mathrm{full}}|\leq z_{1-\alpha^{\mathrm{sign}}/2}. \end{cases} \tag{22}\]

The proof of uniform validity is identical to the proof for (14), with \(\widehat{y}_{0,n}\) replaced by \(\widehat{B}_n\). The sign-pretest error is controlled because \(\widehat{B}_n-B_n=\widehat{y}_{0,n}-\overline{y}_{0,n}\), and the sign-specific sets in (19) are valid confidence sets for \(\psi_n^{\mathrm{full},+}\) and \(\psi_n^{\mathrm{full},-}\). Thus, over parameter sequences with \(B_n\neq0\),

\[ \liminf_{n\to\infty} \inf_{\theta\in\Theta:\,B_n\neq0} \mathbb{P}_\theta\left( \rho_n^{\mathrm{full}}\in C_{1-\alpha,n}^{\mathrm{full}} \right) \geq 1-\alpha. \]

Appendix

Asymptotic equivalence of the Fieller and delta-method intervals as \(|\sqrt{n} \widehat{y}_{0,n} / \widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}|\to\infty\)

We show that the positive-denominator part of (9), written in the form \[ \left| \rho_n^+ - \frac{\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}}{\widehat{y}_{0,n}} \right| \leq \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}}{\sqrt n\,|\widehat{y}_{0,n}|} \left( \left|1+\rho_n^+\right|\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n} + \widehat{\sigma}_{1,n} \right), \] is asymptotically equivalent to the delta-method interval in (6) as \(|\sqrt{n} \widehat{y}_{0,n} / \widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}|\to\infty\). To compare the two intervals, define

\[ \begin{gathered} d = \left|\rho_n^+ - \widehat{\rho}_n^+\right|, \qquad b = \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}}{\sqrt n\,|\widehat{y}_{0,n}|}, \\ h = \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}}{\sqrt n\,|\widehat{y}_{0,n}|} \left( \left|1+\widehat{\rho}_n^+\right|\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n} + \widehat{\sigma}_{1,n} \right). \end{gathered} \]

When \(\widehat{y}_{0,n}>0\), the delta-method interval in (6) can be written as the set \(\{\rho_n^+: d\leq h\}\). Any value in the Fieller set satisfies

\[ \begin{aligned} d &\leq \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}}{\sqrt n\,|\widehat{y}_{0,n}|} \left( \left|1+\rho_n^+\right|\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n} + \widehat{\sigma}_{1,n} \right) \\ &\leq h + b \left| \left|1+\rho_n^+\right| - \left|1+\widehat{\rho}_n^+\right| \right| \\ &\leq h+bd. \end{aligned} \]

Therefore, whenever \(b<1\),

\[ d\leq \frac{h}{1-b}. \]

This gives the outer inclusion

\[ C_{1-\alpha,n}^{+} \subseteq \left\{ \rho_n^+: d\leq\frac{h}{1-b} \right\}. \]

Conversely, the reverse triangle inequality gives \[ \left|1+\rho_n^+\right| \geq \left|1+\widehat{\rho}_n^+\right| - d. \] Therefore the Fieller right-hand side is bounded below by

\[ \begin{aligned} &\frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}}{\sqrt n\,|\widehat{y}_{0,n}|} \left( \left|1+\rho_n^+\right|\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n} + \widehat{\sigma}_{1,n} \right) \\ &\qquad\geq \frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}}{\sqrt n\,|\widehat{y}_{0,n}|} \left( \left|1+\widehat{\rho}_n^+\right|\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n} -d\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n} + \widehat{\sigma}_{1,n} \right) \\ &\qquad= h-bd. \end{aligned} \]

If \(d\leq h/(1+b)\), then \(d\leq h-bd\). We therefore have

\[ \left\{ \rho_n^+: d\leq\frac{h}{1+b} \right\} \subseteq C_{1-\alpha,n}^{+}. \]

The Fieller set is thus squeezed between a contraction and expansion of the delta-method interval. Since \(|\sqrt n\,\widehat{y}_{0,n}/\widehat{\sigma}_{0,n}|\to\infty\) implies \(b\to0\), the scale factors \(1/(1+b)\) and \(1/(1-b)\) both converge to one.

The same argument applies to the negative-denominator region after replacing \(\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}/\widehat{y}_{0,n}\) by \(-\widehat{\psi}_n^{\mathrm{ATE}}/\widehat{y}_{0,n}\) and \(\left|1+\rho_n^+\right|\) by \(\left|1-\rho_n^-\right|\).
